### SIGNAL 1: REVENUE-PROFIT DIVERGENCE [Grade A]
**Confidence: HIGH — Multiple independent sources (PSX filings, analyst reports)**

**What the company shows:**
- FY24 Revenue: PKR 160 billion (up ~15% YoY)
- 9M FY25 Revenue: PKR 134.7 billion (up 12% YoY)
- H1 FY25 Revenue: up 16.17% YoY
- Q1 FY25 Revenue: PKR 43.4 billion (up 9% YoY)

**What the signals reveal:**
- FY24 PAT: PKR 6.37 billion — SIGNIFICANTLY lower than prior year
- 9M FY24 PAT: Declined 52% despite 15% revenue growth
- H1 FY25 PAT: Declined 26.45% despite 16.17% revenue growth
- 9M FY25 PAT: Declined 9.51% despite 12% revenue growth

**The Divergence:**
Revenue and profit are moving in OPPOSITE DIRECTIONS. Every period shows revenue up and profit down. The gap between top-line growth and bottom-line performance is widening.

**Root Causes Identified:**
- Cost of sales surging (up 10.8% to 54% depending on period)
- Finance costs significantly increased
- Super tax rate increased
- "Other income" declined sharply (H1 FY25: PAT decline primarily from lower other income)

**Inference [Grade A]:**
The company may be relying on "other income" (likely investment income, associate companies) to sustain profits rather than core operations. When other income declines, the true operational weakness is exposed. This is a structural dependency, not a one-off event.

**What Would Change My Mind:**
If FY25 full-year results show PAT growth alongside revenue growth, suggesting the margin compression was cyclical (interest rate driven). The projected Q4 FY25 improvement (PKR 1.8B PAT, up from PKR 1.02B) would be a datapoint in this direction — but it's driven by lower taxes and finance costs, not better margins.

### SIGNAL 3: COTTON COST PARADOX [Grade B]
**Confidence: MODERATE — Multiple macro sources, company-specific impact inferred**

**What the macro data shows:**
- Global cotton prices DECLINING: Cotlook A Index down 13.4% in 2024/25, projected to hit 60 cents/lb by end-2025
- Pakistan domestic cotton prices RISING: Up from 73 to 82 cents/lb (PKR 17,000 to 19,000/maund)
- Pakistan cotton production DECLINING: 4.8M bales vs 15M needed — forces massive imports
- Global oversupply: Production exceeding consumption by 800,000 tonnes

**The Paradox:**
Global cotton is getting cheaper, but Pakistani mills are paying MORE. This creates a double squeeze:
1. Input costs are rising domestically even as global prices fall
2. Competitors sourcing from global markets (India, Bangladesh) get cheaper cotton while Pakistani mills don't

**Impact on Nishat Mills:**
- Cost of sales surged 10.8-54% in different periods — consistent with rising input costs
- Margin stabilization at ~11% despite revenue growth suggests cost absorption is maxed out
- Pakistan needs to import 5.6M bales to supplement domestic shortfall — import costs hit mills directly

**What Would Change My Mind:**
If Nishat Mills has significant forward purchasing contracts (hedging) that lock in lower global prices. Their annual report should detail commodity hedging policy. If they're hedged, the cost impact is delayed, not eliminated.

### SIGNAL 4: ENERGY COST TRAP [Grade B]
**Confidence: MODERATE — Company disclosures + industry data**

**What the company says:**
- 38MW solar installed, 4MW more planned
- Evaluating steam boiler and battery storage
- "Focus on energy efficiency to maintain competitive edge"

**What the signals show:**
- FY25 fuel mix: 83% grid/RLNG/furnace oil, only 7% solar
- Weighted average fuel cost: PKR 35-38/kWh
- Annual electricity requirement: ~50MW
- Government base tariff for FY26: PKR 34/unit
- New "Roshan Maeshat" package: PKR 22.98/unit for industries (effective Nov 2025-Oct 2028)

**The Divergence:**
Nishat heavily promotes its solar investment (38MW is impressive on paper), but solar provides only 7% of its energy. The company remains 83% dependent on expensive grid power. At PKR 35-38/kWh, energy is a major cost driver that 4MW of additional solar won't meaningfully address.

**Silver Lining:**
The Roshan Maeshat package at PKR 22.98/unit (effective Nov 2025) could reduce energy costs by ~35-40% IF Nishat qualifies and increases consumption. This is a potential margin recovery catalyst that is NOT yet reflected in current financials.

**What Would Change My Mind:**
If the Roshan Maeshat package benefit shows up in Q2-Q3 FY26 results as a measurable margin improvement, the energy cost thesis weakens significantly.

### SIGNAL 6: EXPANSION DURING STRESS [Grade B]
**Confidence: MODERATE — Company announcements**

**What the company is doing simultaneously:**
1. Adding 3,000 rotors for open-end yarn (expanding capacity)
2. Building a new workwear garments unit
3. Launching Corduroy fabric production
4. Commissioning 35 new Toyota looms for filament fabrics
5. Establishing subsidiary in Turkey
6. Setting up liaison office in Bangladesh
7. Incorporating company in UK (via UAE subsidiary)
8. Investing PKR 5 billion in Nishat Sutas Dairy Limited
9. Adding 4MW solar + evaluating battery storage

**While simultaneously:**
- Profit declining 10-52% across all periods
- Gross margins collapsed to 10-12%
- Issued PKR 7 billion Sukuk for working capital (suggesting cash is tight)

**The Signal:**
The company is expanding aggressively across multiple fronts while its core profitability is under severe pressure. The PKR 7B Sukuk issuance for working capital suggests the expansion is straining liquidity. This pattern — aggressive expansion during margin compression — often precedes either a dramatic turnaround or a cash flow crisis.

**The Nuance:**
Some of these investments (dairy, Turkey/UK subsidiaries) are diversification plays that won't help core textile margins. Others (workwear, technical fabrics, Corduroy) are margin-improvement plays. The question is whether the company can fund all of these simultaneously.

**What Would Change My Mind:**
If the PKR 7B Sukuk was a temporary bridge and the company's cash flow from operations is sufficient to fund expansion without additional debt. Check debt-to-equity ratio trend.

## MANAGEMENT MEETING INTERROGATION BRIEF

### Top 10 Questions — Ranked by Intelligence Value

---

**Q1: "Can you walk us through the trajectory of your gross margins from 33% to 11%? What are the three biggest drivers, and which of them do you expect to reverse?"**
- **Why it matters:** Tests whether management understands the margin compression causality or just attributes it to "market conditions"
- **What to listen for:** Hedging language ("market-driven", "temporary"), lack of specificity, inability to quantify each driver's contribution
- **Red flag response:** "It's an industry-wide phenomenon" — because Interloop appears to be performing better, so it's NOT purely industry-wide
- **Good answer:** Specific decomposition: "X% from cotton, Y% from energy, Z% from product mix shift, and we expect cotton to reverse by Q2 FY26 because of forward contracts"

---

**Q2: "Your solar installation is 38MW but contributes only 7% of energy. What would it take to get solar to 30% of your energy mix, and what's the timeline?"**
- **Why it matters:** 83% grid dependency at PKR 35-38/kWh is a major cost anchor. Tests whether solar is a PR story or a genuine cost reduction strategy
- **What to listen for:** Vague timelines, capital constraints, regulatory barriers
- **Red flag response:** "We're continuously evaluating" — suggests no concrete plan
- **Good answer:** "We need X MW more, at a cost of PKR Y billion, and we've planned this in phases: Z MW by FY27, W MW by FY28"

---

**Q3: "You've issued a PKR 7 billion Sukuk for working capital while simultaneously investing PKR 5 billion in dairy and building subsidiaries in Turkey, UK, and Bangladesh. How is this being funded without straining core operations?"**
- **Why it matters:** Tests capital allocation discipline and whether the company is overleveraging
- **What to listen for:** Inability to separate funding sources, mixing operational and investment cash flows, or revealing that expansion is funded by working capital debt
- **Red flag response:** "These are separate decisions with different funding sources" — then press: "Can you show us the specific funding for each?"
- **Good answer:** "The Sukuk covers seasonal working capital. Expansion is funded from retained earnings and specific project finance at X rate"

---

**Q4: "Interloop exports $530M and Gul Ahmed exports $372M. You export $350M. What is your specific plan to close the gap with your closest peer?"**
- **Why it matters:** Directly challenges the "market leader" positioning. Tests whether management has a competitive response plan
- **What to listen for:** Pivot to domestic market (legitimate if quantified), dismissal of the comparison, or acknowledgment with no plan
- **Red flag response:** "We compete in different segments" — then follow up: "Then where are you the leader in your segment?"
- **Good answer:** "Our priority is margin per unit rather than volume. Our value-add per unit exported is PKR X vs industry average of PKR Y"

---

**Q5: "Your revenue grew 16% but PAT declined 26% in H1 FY25. You attribute this to 'lower other income.' What was this other income, and should we consider it recurring or non-recurring?"**
- **Why it matters:** If "other income" is investment income or associate company dividends, the business may be structurally dependent on non-core income to sustain profitability
- **What to listen for:** Vague descriptions of "other income," reluctance to decompose it, or admission that it includes one-offs they can't replicate
- **Red flag response:** "Other income varies period to period" — because this means core ops alone can't sustain profit levels
- **Good answer:** "Other income was PKR X from [specific source], of which PKR Y is recurring and PKR Z was a one-off"

---

**Q6: "Your cost of sales surged 54% in Q2 FY24. Can you break this down by input — cotton, energy, labor, processing — with the percentage attributable to each?"**
- **Why it matters:** Without decomposition, it's impossible to assess which costs are controllable vs external. A 54% surge suggests something broke
- **What to listen for:** Inability to provide granular data, or attributing everything to "raw materials" without specifics
- **Red flag response:** "Our cost accounting captures blended costs" — suggests they don't track input costs at the granularity needed for control
- **Good answer:** "Cotton accounted for X%, energy Y%, labor Z%. Cotton was up because [specific forward purchase timing]"

---

**Q7: "What is the expected margin impact of the Roshan Maeshat energy package on your FY26 results? Can you quantify it?"**
- **Why it matters:** If the government package delivers PKR 22.98/unit vs current PKR 35-38/unit, this is a 35-40% energy cost reduction. On 50MW demand, this is significant. Tests whether management has modeled the impact
- **What to listen for:** Inability to quantify, uncertainty about eligibility, or contradictions with their own fuel cost data
- **Red flag response:** "We're still evaluating eligibility" — months after the package was announced, this suggests either ineligibility or administrative incompetence
- **Good answer:** "We expect PKR X billion annual savings starting from [date], which translates to Y basis points of margin improvement"

---

**Q8: "You're entering Corduroy, workwear, and technical fabrics simultaneously. What is the expected margin profile of each vs your current product mix?"**
- **Why it matters:** If these new product lines have lower margins than current products, expansion could further compress margins. If higher, they're the turnaround catalyst
- **What to listen for:** Whether management can articulate margin expectations or is expanding for revenue growth without margin clarity
- **Red flag response:** "We expect healthy margins" — without quantification, this is meaningless
- **Good answer:** "Corduroy margins are X%, workwear is Y%, vs our current blended margin of 11%. We expect the mix shift to bring blended margins to Z% by FY27"

---

**Q9: "Your Nishat Sutas Dairy investment is PKR 5 billion and growing. What is the strategic rationale for a textile company investing in dairy, and what are the returns to date?"**
- **Why it matters:** Conglomerate diversification into unrelated industries during core business stress is a classic value-destruction pattern
- **What to listen for:** Vague "synergy" claims, inability to show returns, or admission that dairy is early-stage with no current returns
- **Red flag response:** "Dairy is a long-term strategic investment" — how long-term, and who decided this while margins were collapsing?
- **Good answer:** "The dairy investment generates PKR X annual revenue, Y profit, and is strategically tied to [specific rationale]. Our return on investment is Z% and we expect break-even by [date]"

---

**Q10: "Your stock is up 81% this year while your profits declined. What do you believe the market is pricing in, and is that expectation realistic?"**
- **Why it matters:** If management doesn't understand why their stock is up, they can't manage to those expectations. If the rally is driven by sector rotation or macro factors (interest rate cuts), a reversal is possible
- **What to listen for:** Whether management acknowledges the disconnect, attributes it to "market confidence," or has specific catalysts in mind
- **Red flag response:** "The market recognizes our fundamentals" — but the fundamentals show declining profits, so this is contradictory
- **Good answer:** "We believe the market is pricing in our capacity expansion coming online in [date], expected finance cost reduction of PKR X billion, and energy cost savings from [package]"
